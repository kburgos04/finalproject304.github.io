Biometric Technology

Fingerprint & Facial Recognition

1. Introduction of the Technology

Biometric technology enables identification and verification of individuals using unique biological traits. Fingerprint and facial recognition are two of the most common biometric systems today.

We chose these technologies because of their rising use in everyday devices and critical infrastructure. They are innovative yet raise complex social and ethical concerns.

This image depicts different types of fingerprint scanning.

2. Applications of the Technology

Smartphone Security

Modern smartphones use fingerprint and facial recognition for authentication, app access, and secure payments. These methods are faster and often more secure than traditional passwords.

Border Control and Law Enforcement

Facial recognition is increasingly used at airports and by police forces. It helps match travelers to passports and identify people of interest in public surveillance footage.

This video provides an explanation of how facial recognition systems work.

3. Complexities and Social Challenges

Despite its promise, biometric tech brings serious challenges. Systems can be biasedâ€”facial recognition algorithms often perform poorly on darker skin tones. This leads to disproportionate misidentification and injustice.

Privacy is also a major concern. Users may not know how or where their biometric data is stored, or who has access to it. Once leaked, biometric data can't be changed like a password.

4. Critical Discussion of the Technology

Social and Political Implications

Biometrics can be used for mass surveillance, raising civil rights concerns. Several governments have paused or banned public use of facial recognition due to fears of abuse and lack of transparency.

Ethical Lenses

Utilitarian: Biometrics benefit the majority by increasing convenience and safety.

Deontological: Even if useful, collecting biometric data without consent violates moral rights.

Ethics of Care: Focuses on vulnerable groups who may suffer more from misidentification or surveillance.

5. Future Recommendations

  • Technological: Improve algorithms to reduce bias and error rates across diverse demographics.
  • Social: Increase user awareness and give options to opt-in or opt-out of biometric systems.
  • Policy: Create strong privacy laws governing biometric data use, storage, and accountability.

With thoughtful design and regulation, biometric technology can support society without compromising individual freedoms.
